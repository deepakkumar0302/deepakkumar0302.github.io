Weaker Typhoons and Economic Impact: Evidence from Vietnam

Job market paper

This paper quantifies the short-run economic impacts of low-intensity typhoons, defined as tropical storms and tropical depressions, on local economic activity. We con- struct a commune-by-month panel from 2012 to 2022 by aggregating monthly energy consumption records for residential and non-residential customers across three coastal provinces in Vietnam and merging these data with detailed typhoon track information. We exploit fine spatial variation in exposure, defined by proximity within 15 kilometers of a storm’s path, and estimate dynamic responses using an event-study difference-in- differences design and a stacked difference-in-differences estimator. Communes within 15 kilometers of a tropical storm experience an immediate decline of about 30 percent in energy consumption in the month of landfall, with effects that persist for several months and largely dissipate by month six, while tropical depressions have no detectable effect. The losses are highly localized, concentrated in rural and coastal communes, and larger for non-household customers outside urban areas. Using an estimated elasticity that links a 10 percent decline in energy consumption to a 2.3 percent decline in provin- cial GDP, we estimate that a typical tropical storm in the study region reduces total provincial GDP by roughly 0.2 to 0.3 percent, corresponding to about 45 to 65 million USD in 2022 terms. Finally, we show that night-time lights, a commonly used proxy for local economic activity, fails to detect these disruptions. These findings indicate that standard disaster statistics and remote-sensing based measures miss a meaningful share of the economic costs imposed by lower-intensity but frequent events.

A Gendered Approach to Economic Impacts of Refugee Aid in Kenya

with coauthors · Work in progress

This project studies how large-scale refugee settlements and humanitarian aid reshape host economies in northern Kenya. Using nighttime lights, household data, and a local general equilibrium model, we quantify how transfers to refugees spill over to male- and female-headed households in surrounding communities and how camp design (traditional vs. integrated settlements) affects these outcomes.

Climate-Smart Agriculture and Technology Adoption

Ongoing work · Literature synthesis and empirical applications

I review and extend evidence on the adoption of climate-smart agricultural technologies by smallholder farmers, emphasizing constraints faced by poor and vulnerable households and the role of bundled interventions that combine information, finance, and risk management tools.
